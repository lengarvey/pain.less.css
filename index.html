Painless CSS, The Manifesto

I love the web, and I'm not trying to say that by picking a different css framework than me, you therefore love it less than I. I'm just trying to say that I find its simplicity, flexibility and an infinite number of ways to combine things together, beautiful.

Unfortunately things where getting utterly complex lately, we have to squeeze more and more stuff in our markup and content in order to make the things look great. And that's not an enjoyable process.

Yes, we do automate all everything, and no one really writes HTML by hands anymore (although right at this moment I do), but this process is like a snowball. We have complex CSS frameworks, so we generate our HTML through abstraction layers, and as we automate the templates, we lower our threshold and let the CSS side to get even more complex.

Somewhere in this rush we loose the original simplicity and innocence of the web. Surely you do need all the heavy lifting tools and they are fully justified in many cases. But, in many cases they're not.

I believe that humans are striving for simplicity, I believe that most of the things in HTML already have enough context for us to paint it pretty. 

<button>Hey, I'm a button!</button>
<input type="text" placeholder="I'm totally an input field!">
<button type="submit">I submit stuff</button>

Which gets us to the goal of this project.

The Goal

Lets pretend for a second that the good old HTML, the way our lord and savior Tim Berners-Lee envisioned it, already have enough data and context for us to paint it sanely.

Lets pretend that we can write our HTML in semantical and meaningful structures, just like when we were young and fresh out of the bootcamp. And still, with those simple and innocent structures, get a great and maintainable look.

Lets pretend that a CSS framework can be as painless and simple as a plain css-reset. You just throw it in and everything looks awesome. No ties to a vendor, no three classes and four extra elements scaffolding. It's just there when you need it.

And lets pretend that by doing so, we create space for the good things, the things that matter. Because making things look decently out of the box is not a luxury, it's a necessity. As a good foundation gives you better chances to make it.
